# Example 1
> You're thinking of the **Aitken Bible** (also known as the "Bible of the Revolution"), printed by Philadelphia publisher Robert Aitken in 1782. This was the first complete English-language Bible printed in North America (specifically in what had become the independent United States).

In January 1781, Aitken petitioned the Congress of the Confederation (the body operating under the Articles of Confederation before the U.S. Constitution) for support to publish it, mentioning his intent to produce "a neat Edition of the Holy Scriptures for the use of schools." Congress appointed a committee to oversee progress and had congressional chaplains review it for accuracy.

On September 12, 1782, Congress passed a resolution commending Aitken's work as "pious and laudable," noting it served "the interest of religion" and demonstrated "the progress of arts in this country." They recommended the edition to U.S. inhabitants and authorized Aitken to publish their endorsement (which he included in the Bible itself). This makes it the only Bible edition ever to receive such a congressional endorsement/resolution.

It's often described in historical contexts as having been used for reading instruction and moral education in early American schools (the Bible was commonly a key text for literacy before widespread secular textbooks), which aligns with why people sometimes refer to it as the "first textbook endorsed by Congress." However, Congress did not print it, fund it, mandate its use in schools, or declare it an official textbook—claims like that are overstated or inaccurate, as fact-checks and sources from the Library of Congress confirm. The endorsement was more symbolic and patriotic (highlighting American independence from British printing monopolies) than a formal adoption for education.

If you're recalling it in the context of early American education or debates about religion in schools, that's the one! It's a fascinating piece of history from right after the Revolutionary War.


# Example 2
> The "inscription" you're referring to in the **Aitken Bible** (1782) is the printed congressional resolution/endorsement that Robert Aitken included in copies of his Bible (typically on a leaf following the title page or in the front matter, not literally handwritten inside the cover like a personal inscription, but as an official printed recommendation).

Congress authorized Aitken to publish their resolution "in the manner he shall think proper," so he incorporated the full text into the Bible itself as a commendation.

The full text of the congressional resolution (passed September 12, 1782, and signed by Secretary Charles Thomson) reads as follows:

> Whereupon,  
> RESOLVED,  
> THAT the United States in Congress assembled highly approve the pious and laudable undertaking of Mr. Aitken, as subservient to the interest of religion, as well as an instance of the progress of arts in this country, and being satisfied from the above report of his care and accuracy in the execution of the work, they recommend this edition of the Bible to the inhabitants of the United States, and hereby authorize him to publish this Recommendation in the manner he shall think proper.

This endorsement praised Aitken's work for supporting religion and showcasing American printing capabilities post-Revolution (no more reliance on British imports). It was based on a positive report from congressional chaplains William White and George Duffield, who verified the Bible's accuracy.

Some sources note slight minor variations in phrasing across reproductions (e.g., "highly approve" vs. "highly approv[ing]"), but the above is the standard, widely cited version from historical records like the Journals of Congress and reproductions.

If you're thinking of a different kind of inscription (like a personal note or something else inside surviving copies), those would vary by individual Bible—some have owner inscriptions—but the famous one tied to Congress is this printed resolution.
